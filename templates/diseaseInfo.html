Plant Disease Profiles
Bacterial Spot
Danger Level: Moderate Commonness: Common
What is it?
Bacterial spot is a bacterial infection, often caused by Xanthomonas species, that affects leaves and fruit of many plants, especially tomatoes and peppers.
How does it affect the plant?
It causes small dark, water-soaked spots on leaves that enlarge and turn brown. Fruits may develop raised, corky spots. Overall, it reduces yield and plant vigor.
History
First identified in the early 1900s in the United States, it spread rapidly through contaminated seeds and thrives in warm, humid climates.
How to treat
  • Remove infected leaves.
  • Use copper-based bactericides.
  • Improve air circulation.
  • Avoid overhead watering.
  • Use disease-free seeds.
Ease of Treatment: Medium (bacteria are persistent).
Typical Cure Time: 2-4 weeks, depending on severity.
Early Blight
Danger Level: Moderate-High  |  Commonness: Very Common
What is it?
Early blight is a fungal disease caused by Alternaria solani.
How does it affect the plant?
It creates brown leaf spots with concentric rings (a “bullseye” pattern). It can defoliate plants, weaken stems, and damage fruit.
History
Recorded since the 1800s, early blight is now widespread in all major tomato- and potato-growing regions worldwide.
How to treat
  • Remove infected leaves.
  • Apply fungicides (chlorothalonil, copper, or biofungicides).
  • Rotate crops regularly.
  • Mulch soil to reduce splash infection.
Ease of Treatment: Medium.
Typical Cure Time: 2-3 weeks with consistent management.
Late Blight
Danger Level: Very High  |  Commonness: Common in cool, wet climates
What is it?
Late blight is caused by Phytophthora infestans, the same pathogen responsible for the historic Irish Potato Famine.
How does it affect the plant?
It causes large brown lesions, white fuzzy mold on leaf undersides, rapid collapse of foliage, and rotting of fruits or tubers.
History
In the 1840s, late blight caused massive potato crop failures in Ireland, leading to famine and migration. It remains a major threat to potatoes and tomatoes.
How to treat
  • Immediately remove and destroy infected plants.
  • Apply strong, preferably systemic fungicides.
  • Avoid overhead watering and wet foliage.
  • Grow resistant cultivars when possible.
Ease of Treatment: Difficult — spreads extremely fast.
Typical Cure Time: No true cure; focus on prevention and containment.
Leaf Mould
Danger Level: Low-Moderate  |  Commonness: Common in greenhouses
What is it?
Leaf mould is a fungal disease, usually caused by Fulvia fulva (also known as Cladosporium fulvum), mainly affecting tomatoes.
How does it affect the plant?
It starts as yellow spots on the top of leaves with olive-green fuzzy mold on the underside. Leaves may curl and eventually drop.
History
Known since the early 1900s, it is especially prevalent in humid greenhouse conditions where air movement is poor.
How to treat
  • Increase ventilation and air flow.
  • Reduce humidity.
  • Apply fungicides as needed.
  • Remove and destroy affected leaves.
Ease of Treatment: Easy-Medium.
Typical Cure Time: 1-3 weeks.
Target Spot
Danger Level: Moderate  |  Commonness: Moderately Common
What is it?
Target spot is a fungal disease caused by Corynespora cassiicola.
How does it affect the plant?
It creates round spots with light centers and dark rings, giving a target-like appearance. Severe cases can cause significant leaf drop.
History
First documented on rubber trees, target spot is now known to infect tomatoes, peppers, and many tropical plants.
How to treat
  • Remove infected leaves.
  • Improve plant spacing and airflow.
  • Apply appropriate fungicides.
Ease of Treatment: Medium.
Typical Cure Time: 2-3 weeks.
Yellow Leaf Curl Virus (TYLCV)
Danger Level: Very High  |  Commonness: Very Common in warm climates
What is it?
Yellow leaf curl virus is a viral disease transmitted mainly by whiteflies, particularly damaging to tomatoes.
How does it affect the plant?
Infected plants show stunted growth, curled yellow leaves, reduced fruit set, and general decline.
History
Discovered in the mid-1900s, TYLCV has caused major outbreaks since the 1990s due to the global spread of whiteflies.
How to treat
There is no cure for infected plants. Management focuses on:
  • Removing and destroying infected plants.
  • Controlling whiteflies (nets, insecticides, sticky traps).
  • Planting virus-resistant varieties.
  • Using reflective mulches to deter whiteflies.
Ease of Treatment: Difficult (virus cannot be cured).
Typical Cure Time: No cure — prevention and vector control only.
Mosaic Virus (e.g., TMV, CMV)
Danger Level: Moderate  |  Commonness: Very Common
What is it?
Mosaic viruses are a group of plant viruses that cause mosaic-pattern discoloration on leaves. Examples include Tobacco Mosaic Virus (TMV) and Cucumber Mosaic Virus (CMV).
How does it affect the plant?
Leaves develop mottled light and dark green patches, become distorted, and plants grow slowly with reduced yields.
History
Tobacco Mosaic Virus (TMV) was the first virus ever discovered, identified in 1892, making it historically important in virology.
How to treat
There is no cure for mosaic viruses, so management focuses on prevention:
  • Remove and destroy infected plants.
  • Sanitize tools and hands regularly.
  • Control insect vectors like aphids.
  • Plant resistant varieties when available.
Ease of Treatment: Difficult — virus can persist on surfaces and plant debris.
Typical Cure Time: No cure; focus on preventing spread.
Septoria Leaf Spot
Danger Level: Moderate  |  Commonness: Common
What is it?
Septoria leaf spot is a fungal disease caused by Septoria lycopersici, commonly affecting tomatoes.
How does it affect the plant?
It causes small circular spots with dark borders and light centers on leaves. Severe infections cause heavy leaf drop, but rarely kill the plant outright.
History
Described since the 1800s, it is common in humid regions and home gardens.
How to treat
  • Remove and destroy infected leaves.
  • Apply fungicides such as chlorothalonil or copper-based products.
  • Improve spacing between plants for better airflow.
  • Avoid overhead watering to keep leaves dry.
Ease of Treatment: Medium.
Typical Cure Time: 2-4 weeks.
Spider Mites
(Technically a pest, not a disease)
Danger Level: Moderate  |  Commonness: Very Common
What is it?
Spider mites are tiny arachnid pests that feed on plant sap, often found on the undersides of leaves.
How does it affect the plant?
Leaves develop yellow speckles, plants may be covered with fine webbing, and heavy infestations can weaken or kill plants.
History
Spider mites are widespread across global agriculture and home gardens, with outbreaks often linked to hot, dry conditions.
How to treat
  • Wash leaves with a strong jet of water.
  • Use insecticidal soap or neem oil.
  • Release predatory mites that eat spider mites.
  • Increase humidity around plants.
Ease of Treatment: Easy-Medium.
Typical Cure Time: 1-2 weeks for mild cases; 3-5 weeks for heavy infestations.
